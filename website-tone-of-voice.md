# Writing Style Guide

Reference: [Nate's Newsletter](https://natesnewsletter.substack.com/)

---

## Voice

Confident, fast, direct. The voice of someone who has already done the thinking and is delivering conclusions — not building up to them. Treat the reader as a peer, not a student. No hedging. No moralizing. Diagnose.

---

## Openings

Start mid-argument. The first sentence is a claim, a collision of facts, or a direct negation of conventional wisdom. Never contextual. Never warm-up.

**Do:** "I signed up to learn. I did not expect to ship."
**Don't:** "In this post, I want to explore what I learned during my Bildungsurlaub week."

---

## Sentence rhythm

Short declarative sentences as hammers. Longer sentences supply proof or context. Run two or three short sentences back-to-back for staccato percussion, then break the pattern with a pivot.

> "I hadn't planned to build anything. It just became obvious how."

Compression first, release second.

---

## The negative definition

Use "It isn't X, it's Y" to signal a reframe. This is the sharpest transitional move.

> "I didn't go to learn to code. I went to pick up some syntax. Those aren't the same thing."

---

## Paragraph length

Short. Very short. 1–3 sentences is the target. Break before it feels natural to break. White space is part of the pace.

---

## Concrete details

Name the specific thing. Course name, day number, what the instructor was covering. Abstractions earn their place only if immediately grounded by a fact.

**Do:** "On day four, while the instructor was covering RAG, I opened a new project folder."
**Don't:** "Partway through the course, I started building something."

---

## Use of "I"

Sparse and intentional. Use it to mark a choice, a realization, or an authorial signal — not autobiography for its own sake. "I" is a navigator, not a character.

---

## Transitions

No "furthermore" or "however." A line break and a new short declarative is a transition. Section headers name the concept as a proper noun: "The 40-hour wall," not "My experience with time pressure."

---

## Vocabulary

Mid-register. Short Anglo-Saxon words in the punchy sentences. Industry terms used accurately and without apology — no need to explain "RAG" to this audience. No jargon as display.

---

## What to avoid

- Warm-up paragraphs that restate the title
- Sentences that hedge ("I think," "it seems like," "perhaps")
- Lists where prose rhythm would be sharper
- Orphaned transitional sentences ("That's the thing X did that I didn't expect.")
- Ending a section on a vague observation — end on the sharpest fact or the clearest implication

---

## Role and scope

State who you are and what you were trying to solve in the first paragraph — not as biography, but as orientation. The reader needs to know the vantage point immediately. PM voice: what decision did this unlock, who changed their behavior, what was the product outcome. Not analyst voice: what pattern did I observe.

---

## The discovery-first structure

Lead with the insight, not the setup. The best original idea in the piece belongs in the top third. Everything else — context, method, caveats — serves it.

---

## Endings

Don't end on an open question. End on the sharpest implication of what you found — even if partial, even if the full answer is unknown. An open question signals you stopped before the conclusion. A partial answer with acknowledged limits signals intellectual honesty *and* judgment.

---

## Disclaimers

Never footnote your own writing defensively. If context is needed, fold it into the opening as a statement of vantage point: "I'm a PM on a sponsored ads product at a classifieds platform." That's it. No "views are my own" boilerplate.

---

## The recruiter test

A strong piece of writing for a senior PM audience should make the following legible without being stated explicitly:
- What problem you were given (scope)
- What you discovered (judgment)
- What you did about it (agency)
- Who else was affected (cross-functional reach)

If any of these four are invisible, the piece undersells you.
